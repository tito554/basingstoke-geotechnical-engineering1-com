The Cretaceous chalk which underlies much of Basingstoke presents a particular challenge for site investigation, as subtle dissolution features and variable weathering profiles can create unseen voids or soft zones that boreholes alone may miss. Seismic tomography, combining both refraction and reflection methods, provides a continuous subsurface image that maps the transition from Grade VI weathered chalk down to competent Grade I/II rock, a distinction critical for foundation design in the London Basin margin. Our field crews deploy 24-channel seismographs with geophone spacings optimised for the 15 to 40 metre depth targets typical of commercial developments in Basingstoke, where the Upper Chalk Formation tends to show higher fracture density than the Middle Chalk below. For sites near the River Loddon floodplain, where alluvial gravels overlie the chalk, we often supplement the seismic lines with CPT soundings to correlate the geophysical boundaries with cone resistance profiles, and on larger industrial plots we integrate MASW surveys to derive Vs30 values for seismic site classification under BS EN 1998-1.

Seismic refraction tomography resolves chalk weathering grades and void-prone zones that conventional borehole spacing can overlook, delivering continuous ground models for Basingstoke sites.

Methodology and scope

On Basingstoke's Basing View business park redevelopments, our technicians observed that refraction first-break picking becomes ambiguous where the water table saturates the upper chalk, a condition common after winter recharge when the aquifer rises to within 3 metres of the surface. Our processing workflow therefore applies both the Generalised Reciprocal Method and tomographic inversion, cross-checking the velocity model against borehole log data to resolve the true bedrock surface rather than the geophysical refractor. Reflection processing on the same spreads can identify flint bands and hardgrounds within the chalk sequence, features which act as preferential fracture horizons and influence grout take during foundation treatment. The seismic source, typically a 12 kg sledgehammer on a strike plate or an accelerated weight drop for deeper targets, generates sufficient energy to image to 60 metres in Basingstoke's geology. Where access permits, we also use crosshole seismic techniques between boreholes to obtain ultra-high-resolution velocity profiles between logged boreholes, a method that has proven valuable for karst hazard assessment on the chalk outcrop.

Local considerations

A speculative office development on the western fringe of Basingstoke encountered a 2-metre-wide dissolution pipe infilled with soft clay, missed entirely by a 20-metre grid of cable percussion boreholes. The feature was only identified because the seismic refraction line showed an abrupt velocity drop from 2200 m/s in competent chalk to 900 m/s over a 5-metre lateral distance, prompting targeted investigation. Such dissolution features, formed by Cenozoic weathering along joints in the Seaford Chalk, are a recognised geohazard across the Basingstoke area and can lead to differential settlement if undetected. Inadequate ground investigation that relies solely on point-source data carries a material risk of construction delays, foundation redesign, and remedial grouting costs that can exceed the original groundworks budget. A continuous seismic tomography survey, calibrated with at least two borehole logs, reduces this uncertainty by imaging the lateral extent of anomalous zones before the piling rig arrives on site.

MASW and Vs30 Site Classification

Multichannel analysis of surface waves for shear wave velocity profiling to 30 metres depth, providing the Vs30 value required for seismic site classification under BS EN 1998-1. Data acquired with 4.5 Hz geophones and processed using dispersion curve inversion.

Frequently asked questions

What depth can seismic refraction tomography reach in the chalk around Basingstoke?

With a 115-metre spread and a 14 kg sledgehammer source on a strike plate, we typically achieve 45 to 60 metres of penetration in dry chalk conditions. Penetration reduces when the chalk is saturated, as the P-wave velocity contrast between weathered and competent chalk diminishes. For deeper targets beyond 60 metres, we use an accelerated weight drop source and may combine refraction with reflection processing to image flint bands and marl seams at depths of 80 metres or more.

How much does a seismic tomography survey cost for a typical Basingstoke development site?

For a standard commercial plot in Basingstoke requiring 3 to 5 seismic refraction lines with tomographic processing, the survey cost ranges from approximately £2,170 to £4,100 depending on the number of line-metres, source type, and whether MASW or crosshole testing is included. A detailed quotation is provided following a desk study review of the site geology and existing ground investigation data.

Can seismic reflection surveys detect dissolution features in the chalk?

Seismic reflection can image high-impedance boundaries such as flint bands and hardgrounds within the chalk, but dissolution pipes and small voids are more reliably detected by refraction tomography, which responds to the velocity reduction in the infill material. Crosshole seismic tomography between boreholes provides the highest resolution for mapping individual dissolution features, with velocity anomalies of 30% or more relative to intact chalk clearly identifiable.

What is the difference between seismic refraction and MASW for site classification?

Seismic refraction provides a P-wave velocity model of the subsurface, which is used to map geological boundaries and assess rippability, while MASW (Multichannel Analysis of Surface Waves) directly measures shear wave velocity (Vs), the parameter needed for seismic site classification to BS EN 1998-1. On a typical Basingstoke site, we acquire both datasets simultaneously using the same geophone spread, processing the refraction data for geological interpretation and the surface wave data for the Vs30 calculation.
